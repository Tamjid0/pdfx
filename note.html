Quick Summary

The Bohr model, proposed by Niels Bohr in 1913, describes the atom as a small, positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons that travel in circular orbits around the nucleus. This model was the first to incorporate quantum theory and successfully explained the hydrogen spectrum.

üí° Key Insight: Electrons can only occupy certain allowed orbits with specific energy levels, and they jump between these levels by absorbing or emitting photons.
üìö
Historical Context

Before Bohr's model, the prevailing atomic model was Rutherford's model (1911), which proposed that atoms consisted of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons. However, this model had a critical flaw: according to classical physics, electrons orbiting the nucleus should continuously emit radiation and spiral into the nucleus, causing atoms to collapse.

Niels Bohr resolved this issue by introducing quantum mechanics into atomic structure. His model combined Rutherford's nuclear concept with Planck's quantum theory, creating a revolutionary framework that explained atomic stability and spectral lines.

Key Definitions
Term Definition
Stationary Orbit An orbit in which an electron can revolve without radiating energy. Only certain orbits with specific angular momentum are allowed.
Quantized Energy Energy that exists in discrete packets rather than continuous values. Electrons can only possess certain specific energy values.
Ground State The lowest energy state of an atom, where electrons occupy the innermost available orbits (n=1).
Excited State A higher energy state where one or more electrons occupy orbits further from the nucleus (n>1).
Photon A quantum of electromagnetic radiation with energy E = hŒΩ, emitted or absorbed during electron transitions.

1. Stationary Orbits

Electrons revolve around the nucleus in certain fixed circular paths called stationary orbits or energy shells. In these orbits, electrons do not radiate energy despite their acceleration.

Limitations of Bohr's Model
  1. Multi-electron atoms: The model only works well for hydrogen-like atoms with one electron. It fails to explain spectra of multi-electron atoms.
  2. Fine structure: Cannot explain the fine structure of spectral lines (splitting of lines in magnetic field - Zeeman effect).
  3. Intensity of spectral lines: Doesn't predict relative intensities of spectral lines.
  4. Wave nature of electrons: Treats electrons as particles only, ignoring their wave nature.
  5. Chemical bonding: Cannot explain formation of molecules and chemical bonds.
  6. Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle: Violates the uncertainty principle by assuming definite position and momentum simultaneously.
üí° These limitations led to the development of quantum mechanics and the modern atomic model based on wave functions and probability distributions.
